Longitudinal Pattern

Results to RQ1 are shown in Figure 1. The left y-axis in Figure 1 corresponds to the histogram in the graph, indicating the number of developers (Figure 1a), commits (Figure 1b), and projects (Figure 1c). The right y-axis corresponds to the lines indicating the ratio of female participation.

Overall, all three measurements show a similarly low but steady increase in the female ratio. As presented in Figure 1, in the beginning (2008), female contributors took up less than 3% of all active contributor population and number of commits, and participated in only 5.8% of all projects. The ratio had been relatively stable until 2012, followed by an increase in the next 6 years. By the end of our data, female participation has increased for all three aspects. Female ratio grew to 7.96% among all and 6.0% among core contributors; the ratio of female-authored commits reached 10.98%; and as many as 19.3% projects had at least one female contributor. There is a drastic increase of female commit around 2018 in Figure 1b. We speculate that one of the reasons is the increase in female commits to Ruby projects, of which many are programming course repository with a large number of female participants. After a manual inspection, we found that the top 50 Ruby public projects with the most number of female contributors are all course materials. Moreover, education programs in other ecosystems such as Jupyter Notebook, although not as prevalent as in Ruby, also attracted many female developers.

Shown in Figure 1a, the core female contributor ratio in 2018 is 6.0%, much higher than the 2.3% reported by Canedo et al. [3]. Overall, female ratio among core and among all developers were both around 3% at first and started to diverge in late 2011.
